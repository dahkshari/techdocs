## Make Your Content AI-Ready 

To optimize your content for AI agents, consider the following best practices.

## Hierarchical Structures 

AI agents learn best from content that has structured hierarchy, which helps them organize their generated output. Organize your content using:

* **Page trees and folders:** AI reads page trees, like the ones in online help systems, to understand your content’s hierarchical structure. AI will pay attention to your folder and sub-folder titles to understand what’s in them.  
* **Headings:** Within your pages or documents, H1, H2, H3, etc., enable the agent to understand the document’s structure. Use headings in order, though. Don’t use H1 and then H3 immediately after just because it looks nicer. “Heading skipping” can potentially confuse the agent.  
* **Tables of contents (TOC):** For long web pages, consider adding a TOC at the top. AI agents, like humans, like to get an immediate idea of what’s in a long document and its structure.

## Document Chunking 

LLMs have limited “context windows,” meaning they can only process a finite amount of text at once. So, the bigger the chunk of text, the more likely the AI might get confused or, at the very least, require more time and resources to process.

The challenge is finding a “just right” size of the chunk: too small, and the AI misses the context; too big, AI gets distracted by irrelevant information.

* **Bite-sized pieces:** Break down your content into small, self-contained modules, each addressing a specific task or idea. Aim for small paragraphs, sections, and steps. Make every word count.  
* **Clear topic boundaries:** AI looks for clear topic boundaries to understand the beginning and ends of chunks. Use headings, horizontal rules, or hard-returns to separate paragraphs and chunks.

## Clear, Scannable, Audience-Centric Language 

The fundamentals of good technical writing directly contribute to AI agent performance.

* **Know your audience:** Define your audience somewhere in your document. For example, “This document is for writers who want to make their content AI-ready.” If the AI understands who the content is for, it can better tailor its output when prompted.   
* **Make content scannable:** Short, focused paragraphs (chunks) make it easier for AI to consume. Use not only headings, but bullet points, numbered lists, tables and images. (**Note:** For images, add captions to help the AI agent understand the image.)  
* **Create coherent units of meaning:** Each chunk of information must make sense on its own. For example, when documenting the steps to use a coffee machine to brew a cup of coffee, do not also include in the same instructions steps to clean your coffee mug.   
* **Use clear and simple language:** AI struggles with ambiguity, so use clear, concise, and unambiguous language:  
  * Avoid jargon or define it explicitly when necessary.  
  * Spell out acronyms, at least the first time they’re used.  
  * Maintain consistent naming and terminology throughout the document.

## Standardization Within Your Document 

Consistency in your content is key for effective AI grounding. To this end, implement:

* **Consistent naming conventions:** For example, use the name of the application you’re documenting consistently. Ensure it’s a name that audiences will understand and use in a prompt.  
* **Uniform formatting:** Establish a consistent formatting style for all elements in your document, like headings, lists, or code blocks. For example, if H1 starts out red, bold, 20pt, make sure it is that way throughout your document. This helps AI find those clear topic boundaries.  
* **Precise instructions:** Write step-by-step instructions with clear action verbs and specific object references. To avoid ambiguity, name who is supposed to perform the task.  
* **Effective internal linking:** Use internal linking within your document or site to strategically link internal pages. Internal links build a “knowledge graph” for the AI, showing how different pieces of information are connected. This allows the AI to follow logical paths to answer complex queries. For example, for instructions to log on to an application, include a link for “Resetting your password.”

## Rich Metadata and Context 

Metadata enables intelligent information retrieval by AI agents, improving response accuracy. Add metadata tags or labels to your content, such as:

* Product title and version  
* Audience  
* Keywords

## Ongoing Maintenance 

Continuous improvement ensures content remains valuable for both humans and AI. This includes:

* **Regular review and updates:** Just like software, documentation needs constant maintenance. Schedule regular reviews of your content to ensure they stay relevant.  
* **Incorporate user feedback:** A well-designed AI agent has functionality for user feedback (like rating an answer or submitting comments). Use this feedback to improve your documentation. If a user said the AI gave them a wrong answer, this might point to an error that you can address in your source documentation.  
* **Archiving outdated content:** AI needs to know what’s current and what’s historical. Archive old content by either labeling it as such or moving it to a location the AI doesn’t reference.

By adopting the practices in this article, **technical writers** and **anyone else who writes documentation** can become architects of their company’s knowledge base, ensuring AI agents are referencing only their best content.
